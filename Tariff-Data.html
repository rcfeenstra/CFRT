Tariff Data

A Worldwide Tariff Database (WTD) over 1990-2010 has been prepared for the analysis in CFRT (2022). An earlier version of the database, WTD version 0 (WTD0) was used for our NBER working paper but it is not available in its original form. WTD version 1 (WTD1) makes extensive checks and improvements to WTD0, and was used to obtain the results in CFRT (2022).

WTD1 is available in two datasets,  with documentation and download provided below:

a)  MFN and preferential tariff data by importing country, for each exporting country with a preferential arrangement:  Annual files containing the Most Favored Nation (MFN) and preferential tariff data at the Standard International Trade Classification (SITC) 4-digit level, by importing country and for each exporting country when there is a preferential arrangement.

b)  MFN and preferential tariff data by importing country, for each exporting country and SITC good with positive trade: Annual files containing MFN and preferential tariff data by importing country and for all exporting countries and SITC goods when there is positive trade in that year. This dataset – when restricted to 178 countries – is used to obtain Figures 1-3 in CFRT (2022).

The tariff dataset downloaded from this site should be referenced to:

Caliendo, Lorenzo, Robert Feenstra, John Romalis and Alan M. Taylor. 2023. “Tariff Reductions, Heterogeneous Firms, and Welfare:  Theory and Evidence for the Last Two Decades.” IMF Economic Review, forthcoming.

Documentation

Introduction

During the time period covered by the data, 1990-2010, a number of countries in eastern Europe changed their composition, so that by 1993: Czechoslovakia split into two countries (the Czech Republic and Slovakia); the USSR split into 15 countries (see the list below); and Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia split into 5 and later 6 countries (see the list below). The quantitative model used in CFRT (2022) is based on the EORA multi-region input-output table (https://worldmrio.com), and EORA includes the Czech Republic, Slovakia, the 15 countries of former USSR, and the 6 countries of former Yugoslavia from 1990 on. *For consistency with EORA, we have omitted Czechoslovakia, the USSR and Yugoslavia from the database, and in their place we have imputed the tariffs of the future countries formed from them:* specific details on how that is achieved are provided below.

Countries of the former USSR (15 countries, with ISO codes):

Armenia (ARM), Azerbaijan (AZE), Belarus (BLR), Estonia (EST), Georgia (GEO), Kazakhstan (KAZ), Kyrgyzstan (KGZ), Latvia (LVA), Lithuania (LTU), Moldova (MDA), Russia (RUS), Tajikistan (TJK), Turkmenistan (TKM), Ukraine (UKR), Uzbekistan (UZB)

Countries of the former Yugoslavia (6 countries with ISO codes, and one combined ISO code):

Bosnia and Herzegovina (BIH), Croatia (HRV), Montenegro (MNE), North Macedonia (MKD),
Serbia (SRB), Slovenia (SVN), and a combined ISO code for Serbia and Montenegro (SCG)

(a)  MFN and preferential tariff data by importing country, for each exporting country with a preferential arrangement

eiso – 3-digit string giving the exporting country ISO code

sitc4 – Standard International Trade 4-digit (SITC) classification, rev. 2

t1_nonmfn – a non-MFN ad valorem tariff, in percent 

Description
Listed in these files is the importer country code (iiso), exporter country code (eiso), alternative country codes from the UN or WITS2 (icode and ewits), SITC rev. 2, 4-digit code (sitc4), along with the most-favored nation (MFN) tariff t1, the preferential tariff between the countries, t1_pref, and – for a very small number of observations – a non-MFN tariff, t1_nonmfn. The main importers for which a non-MFN tariffs is recorded are the United States and Japan, and in these cases, it is only for a small number of exporting countries: in the United States, for example, these are the Communist countries for which “column 2” tariffs are used rather than the MFN tariff.3 Thus, in most cases the non-MFN tariff t1_nonmfn is missing. Importantly, for each importing country the exporters include all countries for which the importer has a preferential trade arrangement. For these exporters, the preferential tariff t1_pref and the MFN tariff t1 are listed; but exporting countries that have no preferential arrangement with the importer may or may not be listed. The MFN tariff for those exporting countries not listed can be inferred, however, from the MFN tariff listed for that importer and SITC goods. Specifically, if an importing country has no preferential agreements, then the MFN tariff schedule might be listed between that importing country and itself as an exporter,4 or between that importing country and the world (WLD) as an exporter, or between that importing country and specific exporting countries: so in any case, the MFN tariffs are provided. 

The SITC rev. 2, 4-digit classification used in this part (a) dataset has 784 goods. That is smaller than the 818 goods in the SITC rev. 2, 4-digit classification used in WTD0, an earlier version of the data, but a number of those 818 SITC codes ended in 0. Some of those codes ending in zero are not true 4-digit SITC codes, and none of them are used in part (a) of WTD1.

In the Appendix Tables  (provided with the paper downloadable on this site: Caliendo, Feenstra, Romalis, Taylor, November, 2022, forthcoming IMF Economic Review), we list the year for which each country has a tariff schedule available and all the preferential trade agreements that are incorporated into the database. For example, Russia appears with a tariff schedule in 1990-1992, though that schedule is actually the published 1993 schedule for Russia. Estonia and Turkmenistan first appear with published tariff schedules in 1992, with no tariffs in part (a) of the database prior to that year. As explained below, we impute the Estonia and Turkmenistan tariffs in 1990 and 1991 by using the Russia tariff schedule from 1993, and the other countries of eastern Europe are treated in a similar way: before these regions exist as independent countries and publish a tariff schedule, we use the tariff schedule (possibly taken from the future) of another country. 

(b) MFN and preferential tariff data by importing country, for each exporting country and SITC good with positive trade

The tariff data described in (a) was reorganized so that, for each year, it contains the MFN and preferential tariff for each importing country, exporting country and SITC good with a positive trade value based on the COMTRADE database of the United Nations.5 The resulting dataset in part (b) has observations for which there is positive trade value between the countries in that SITC good and year.6 We refer to this dataset as the “base” version.

Description 
These files use the identical importer country code (iiso), exporter country code (eiso) and WITS codes (icode and ewits) that are contained in the tariff files described in part (a), and now country names (importer and exporter) are also introduced. There are slight differences, however, in the SITC 4-digit code (sitc4) and the tariffs as compared to part (a), and also the WTD version from which the tariff is drawn. Each of these items is described below. 

i) SITC codes and Countries
The WTD0 database includes 818 SITC codes, some of which ended in 0, but as noted in part (a), the tariff data in WTD1 uses only 784 codes and does not include any ending in zero. The tariff dataset in part (b) of the WTD1 database, includes some SITC codes ending in 0, in which case the tariffs are obtained from version 0 of the data, WTD0. In addition, there are a few importing countries whose tariffs are drawn from version 0 of the data. In both cases, that is indicated by the indicator variable version=0; otherwise, version=1. 

ii) MFN tariffs
The most-favored nation (MFN) tariff t1 reported in this dataset is drawn from the dataset described in part (a). As mentioned at the outset of this documentation, for certain eastern European countries especially during 1990-1993, we created tariffs before these countries existed as separate entities. 

Specifically, for Russia during 1990-1992 we use the tariffs from the published 1993 tariff schedule, and these tariffs are reported for all exporters and SITC good that the USSR imported in each year 1990-1993. The other 14 countries of the former USSR appear with tariff schedules that might be earlier than 1993: Estonia and Turkmenistan first appear with published tariff schedules in 1992, for example. For the tariff database in part (b), we use the 1993 tariffs of Russia for these other 14 countries during 1990-1993, until these countries published their own schedules; and throughout 1990-1993, these tariffs are reported for all exporters and SITC goods that these countries imported in 1994.

The other countries of eastern Europe are also treated in a similar way: before these regions exist as independent countries and publish their own tariff schedule, we use the tariff schedule of another country. Specifically, for the Czech Republic and Slovakia (which were formed from Czechoslovakia on January 1, 1993), their tariffs in 1990-1995 are taken from the 1992 Czechoslovakia tariff schedule, and these tariffs are reported for those exporters and SITC goods that Czechoslovakia imported in 1993. For Serbia during 1990-1998 we use the tariffs from its published 2001 tariff schedule. For the other former Yugoslavia countries during 1990-1998, their tariffs are also taken from the Serbia tariff schedule of 2001 until they publish their own schedules.

iii) Preferential and non-MFN tariffs
For the 15 former USSR countries that are created as distinct entities during 1990-1993, the preferential tariffs between them are set at zero until each country gains independence and publishes a tariff schedule, in which case tariffs might be applied against the other countries. Similarly, the Czech Republic and Slovakia, which are treated as having zero tariffs against each other when they are part of Czechoslovakia during 1990-1992, and the countries of the former Yugoslavia are treated similarly. 

The non-MFN tariff, t1_nonmfn, is very similar in this dataset and in the dataset described in part (a), except that we cross-checked the non-MFN tariffs for the United States against those reported by the U.S. International Trade Commission, to determine the countries against which non-MFN tariffs are applied each year. 

Finally, in this dataset, two adjustments have been made to the preferential tariff between the countries as compared to part (a). First, we set t1_pref equal to t1 in cases where there is no preferential agreements between the countries.8 Second, for the very small number of observations where t1_nonmfn is reported (which is at least as large as t1) , then we set t1_pref equal to t1_nonmfn . In this way, a value for t1_pref is reported for every observation in the dataset, and t1_pref < t1 for nearly all observations, with the exception of those cases where there is a non-MFN tariff reported, in which case as a matter of convention we choose to set t1_pref = t1_nonmfn > t1. By this convention, t1_pref always reflects the operative tariff between countries.
